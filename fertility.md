
## AMH Bloodtest

AMH serves as a proxy for your ovarian reserve. 

Here's what a result might look like and what it would mean for egg freezing:
Say you get back a result of 2.5 ng/ml (or µg/L — same thing in this context).
That would fall solidly in the normal fertile range. In the fertile phase, AMH typically falls between 1.0 and 8.0 ng/ml. Between 0.4 and 1.0 ng/ml suggests diminished fertility. Below 0.1 ng/ml indicates very low reserve.

But the number only means something relative to your age. A 2.5 at 30 reads differently than a 2.5 at 38. The clinic you eventually consult will interpret it alongside your age to estimate how your ovaries would likely respond to stimulation — roughly how many eggs a single cycle might yield.

Practical translation for egg freezing planning:
A higher AMH (say 3.0+) generally means your ovaries would respond well to stimulation, you'd likely retrieve more eggs per cycle, and you might need only one round. A lower AMH (say 0.8) would suggest fewer eggs per retrieval, possibly needing multiple cycles to bank enough — and potentially more urgency around timing.
